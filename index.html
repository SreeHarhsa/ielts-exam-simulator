IELTS Exam Simulator

Welcome to the IELTS Exam Simulator. This application closely replicates the actual IELTS test experience, including all four sections: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking.

Before you begin, please ensure that:

  • You have 2 hours and 45 minutes available to complete the test
  • You are in a quiet environment without distractions
  • You have headphones or speakers for the Listening section
  • You have a microphone for the Speaking section

You will start with the Listening section (30 minutes), followed by Reading (60 minutes), Writing (60 minutes), and finally Speaking (11-14 minutes).

Listening Section

In this section, you will listen to four recordings of native English speakers and then answer a series of questions.

The recordings will be played ONCE only.

  • Recording 1: A conversation between two people in an everyday social context
  • Recording 2: A monologue set in an everyday social context
  • Recording 3: A conversation between up to four people set in an educational or training context
  • Recording 4: A monologue on an academic subject

You will have 30 minutes to complete this section (plus 10 minutes to transfer your answers to the answer sheet).

Reading Section

In this section, you will read three passages and answer a number of questions.

You should spend about 20 minutes on each passage (60 minutes total).

  • The passages increase in difficulty level
  • Each passage will be followed by 13-14 questions
  • Questions may include multiple choice, identifying information, matching information, sentence completion, and more
  • You may need to identify specific details, main ideas, or the writer's views/claims

You will have 60 minutes to answer all questions for all three passages.

Reading Test

Question: 1 of 40

The Impact of Artificial Intelligence on Modern Society

Artificial intelligence (AI) has rapidly transformed from a specialized research field into a pervasive technology affecting virtually every aspect of society. As algorithms become increasingly sophisticated and data more abundant, both the potential benefits and challenges of AI have expanded dramatically, prompting important discussions about how this technology should be developed and governed.

In the workplace, AI is augmenting human capabilities while simultaneously raising questions about employment displacement. Routine and predictable tasks across industries are increasingly being automated, from manufacturing assembly lines to customer service interactions. According to a 2020 World Economic Forum report, 85 million jobs may be displaced by automation by 2025, while 97 million new roles adapted to the division of labor between humans, machines, and algorithms may emerge. This suggests a profound shift rather than simply a reduction in available work.

Meanwhile, healthcare has emerged as one of the most promising applications of AI. Machine learning algorithms can analyze medical images to detect diseases like cancer with accuracy rivaling or sometimes exceeding human specialists. Natural language processing enables the extraction of relevant information from medical literature and patient records, while predictive analytics can identify individuals at risk for various conditions, potentially enabling earlier interventions. These developments could significantly improve patient outcomes while reducing costs, particularly in regions with limited access to healthcare professionals.

AI is also transforming education by enabling personalized learning experiences tailored to individual students' needs. Adaptive learning platforms can identify knowledge gaps, adjust difficulty levels, and provide targeted feedback, potentially addressing the limitations of traditional one-size-fits-all educational approaches. However, integrating these technologies effectively requires careful consideration of pedagogical approaches and teacher training to ensure technology enhances rather than diminishes the learning experience.

Despite these potential benefits, significant ethical concerns have emerged. Decision-making algorithms deployed in areas like hiring, lending, criminal justice, and healthcare may inadvertently perpetuate or amplify existing social biases if trained on data reflecting historical discrimination. The "black box" nature of many advanced machine learning techniques makes it difficult to understand how decisions are reached, creating challenges for accountability and oversight.

Privacy concerns are equally pressing, as AI systems typically require vast amounts of data to function effectively. The ability to extract insights from seemingly innocuous information, combined with increasingly sophisticated surveillance capabilities, raises questions about appropriate limits on data collection and analysis. Moreover, as facial recognition and other biometric identification technologies become more widespread, balancing security applications with civil liberties has become increasingly challenging.

The global nature of AI development has also introduced geopolitical dimensions to its governance. Different regions have adopted varying approaches, with the European Union emphasizing regulation and individual rights, the United States favoring industry-led initiatives, and China investing heavily in AI as a national strategic priority with fewer restrictions on data collection. These divergent approaches complicate efforts to establish international standards and norms.

Looking ahead, ensuring that AI benefits humanity broadly will require thoughtful collaboration among technologists, policymakers, industry leaders, and civil society. Developing technical standards for safety, robustness, and fairness; establishing appropriate regulatory frameworks; investing in education and training; and fostering inclusive dialogue about how AI should be deployed are all essential components of responsible AI development. By addressing these challenges proactively, societies can work toward harnessing AI's potential while mitigating its risks.

Writing Section

In this section, you will complete two writing tasks.

  • Task 1: Describe visual information (graph, table, chart, or diagram) in your own words. You should write at least 150 words in about 20 minutes.
  • Task 2: Write an essay in response to a point of view, argument, or problem. You should write at least 250 words in about 40 minutes.

Task 2 contributes twice as much as Task 1 to your Writing band score.

You will have 60 minutes to complete both tasks.

Speaking Section

In this section, you will participate in a recorded interview with the examiner.

The test has three parts:

  • Part 1: Introduction and interview (4-5 minutes) - General questions about yourself and familiar topics
  • Part 2: Long turn (3-4 minutes) - You will be given a topic card and have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes
  • Part 3: Discussion (4-5 minutes) - Further questions connected to the Part 2 topic

The entire Speaking section will take about 11-14 minutes.
